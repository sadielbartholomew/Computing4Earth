## Weather vs. climate

The state of the (lower) atmosphere at a specific location or in a given region, as defined by
quantities such as the air temperature and pressure,
precipitation, wind strength and direction, and covering phenomena occuring there such as sunshine,
rain, snow, wind and mist, can be described by weather or by climate depending on
the timescales under consideration:

* **weather** describes the instantaneous or short-term (on scales of hourly or daily or similar) state;
* whereas **climate** describes the *average state* in the long term (on scales of monthly,
   yearly or decadal, and typically defined over 30 years).

The nature of forecasting for the two concepts highlights this difference:

* Weather forecasting allows us to make day-to-day plans for the near future, such as what to wear and what activities we might do (and allows businesses to make decisions based on the effect on consumer behaviour and production processes, e.g. increased ice cream sales on hot days and increased risk of transport delays or cancellations with heavy snow).
* Climate projections predict future conditions in terms of patterns/trends, such as forecasting average and extremes for related variables - not specific weather phenomena expectations which are impossible to predict this far ahead - over years or more, usually more like many decades ahead. They enable assessment of future risks and inform decisions on requiremets for infratructure and resource management.

So, climate is the average and variability of the weather over long time periods. A nice adage
which is attributed to either Mark Twain or Robert Heinlein is:

> The climate is what we expect; the weather is what we get.

```{note}
Since the atmosphere on its own, and in particular when coupled with the other systems which it interacts with such as the ocean and land surface, is a chaotic dynamical system, where small uncertainties in the initial conditions grow exponentially through coupled non-linear interactions governed by the equations such as the Navier–Stokes equations, weather forecasts cannot be made more than several days in advance with any accuracy.

The sensitive dependence on initial conditions imposes an intrinsic predictability limit, beyond which deterministic forecasts lose accuracy enough for usefulness, and this limit has been identified as around 10 days from theoretical studies and numerical weather prediction experiments quantifying atmospheric error growth.

So, we can't forecast weather conditions more than a few weeks ahead. But that doesn't mean that
we cannot quite accurately (to defined uncertainty) predict the average and the extremes of core atmospheric variables long term - decades or even centuries ahead - this is what climate projections do.
```
